# Clinical Protocols

## Diseases/Issues
### Heart failure exacerbation
- First-line: IV loop diuretic (Lasix). Goal urine output 1-2L/day
- Monitor: daily BMP (creatinine, K+), daily weights, strict I&Os
- Target: return to dry weight before discharge
- CXR on admission and before discharge
- Cardiology consult if: new reduced EF, considering ICD upgrade, hemodynamic instability
- Note: this patient population often has CKD — watch creatinine closely during diuresis
- If LBBB is present on ECG, interpret with caution for signs of acute ischemia and note that it may obscure traditional ECG findings for ACS
- When patient reports strict medication compliance but presents with decompensation, consider dietary indiscretion, silent ischemia, or progression of underlying disease as triggers
- Check Mg and Phos before initiating/continuing IV loop diuresis; replete proactively to prevent arrhythmia
- Set explicit target dry weight and goal UOP on admission; reconsider prior dry-weight target if discharge weights are trending upward across admissions
- Assess GDMT completeness on every admission (MRA, ARNI, SGLT2i, beta-blocker, ACEi/ARB); document gaps and plan for initiation, with explicit triggers if deferred for AKI/hyperkalemia
- For recurrent ADHF admissions, actively investigate precipitant (ischemia, arrhythmia, nonadherence, dietary indiscretion, NSAIDs, infection, PE) rather than defaulting to nonadherence
- If EF ≤35% with LBBB, verify ICD/CRT device status and evaluate CRT-D candidacy
- Document explicit plan for ACEi/ARB (continue/hold/dose-adjust) during aggressive diuresis when Cr is rising
- Order DVT prophylaxis on admission unless bleeding contraindication documented
- Document code status / goals-of-care on admission, especially with declining trajectory or declined revascularization
- For recurrent ADHF admissions, review trend of discharge dry weights across prior admissions — 'dry weight creep' suggests target dry weight should be reconsidered downward
- Order serial troponin q3-6h x2-3 in ADHF patients with LBBB and known significant CAD to exclude type 2 MI/ACS as precipitant
- Order STAT Mg and Phos before continued IV loop diuretic dosing; replete proactively to prevent arrhythmia
- Identify and document the precipitant of exacerbation (ischemia, arrhythmia, infection, nonadherence, dietary indiscretion, NSAIDs, PE)
- Sodium 2g and fluid 2L restriction on admission
- Verify GDMT completeness on every admission: MRA, ARNI, SGLT2i — recurrent admissions with incomplete GDMT warrant urgent cardiology re-engagement
- EF ≤35% + LBBB meets CRT-D criteria on paper — verify device status and flag for cardiology evaluation
- Consider iron studies (ferritin, TSAT) for IV iron candidacy in HFrEF with anemia
- TSH reasonable in recurrent HF decompensation
- Inpatient IV loop diuretic dosing principle: 1-2.5x the home PO dose, given IV; reassess response at 6-12h and escalate to continuous infusion if inadequate
- Resume home PO diuretic 24-48h before discharge once euvolemic
- Anchor diuresis target to last documented dry weight from prior admissions; watch for progressive dry weight creep across admissions as a marker of worsening HF trajectory
- Consider formal cardiology consult for GDMT gaps (no ARNI/MRA/SGLT2i) or recurrent admissions (≥3 in 18 months), even if EF is known and patient is hemodynamically stable
- ACEi/ARB hold parameters during diuresis: SBP <100, Cr rise >30% from baseline, or K+ >5.0
- Beta-blocker hold parameters: SBP <90, HR <55, or signs of low-output state; reduce rather than discontinue when possible
- When transitioning IV → PO loop diuretic, consolidate to once-daily outpatient regimen where possible for adherence; document rationale for any BID outpatient regimen
- For patients on uptitrated loop diuretic + ACEi + new KCl at discharge, mandatory PCP BMP within 7 days (not 7-14)

### Sepsis / bacteremia
- Blood cultures x2 before antibiotics
- Infectious disease consult for: Staph aureus bacteremia, fungemia, unclear source
- Antibiotic choice: narrow based on cultures as soon as sensitivities available
### Cardiorenal AKI / CKD on diuresis
- Hold metformin during AKI (Cr above baseline) to mitigate lactic acidosis risk
- Nephrology consult trigger: Cr rises >25% above baseline or diuresis stalls
- Daily BMP during active diuresis
### Anemia in HFrEF
- Workup mild anemia with iron studies (ferritin, TSAT) and TSH
- Consider IV iron if iron-deficient (mortality/hospitalization benefit in HFrEF)
### Cardiorenal syndrome / AKI on CKD during diuresis
- Set explicit nephrology consult trigger: Cr rise >25% above baseline OR UOP <1 L/day on adequate diuretic dosing
- Hold metformin when Cr rises into AKI range (lactic acidosis risk); check lactate if hold is delayed
- Document explicit continue/hold/dose-adjust plan for ACEi/ARB before next dose during AKI
- Defer MRA initiation until Cr stable/improving and K <5.0; document the specific trigger for initiation
### Recurrent heart failure admissions
- For patients with ≥2 ADHF admissions in 6-12 months, evaluate for CHF disease management program or transitional care clinic enrollment
- Track dry-weight trend across admissions — upward creep suggests self-management gap warranting social work evaluation
- Revisit code status and goals-of-care discussion in setting of accelerating decompensation, especially if prior revascularization was declined
- Start Part D prior authorization workflow for new GDMT agents (ARNI, SGLT2i) at least 48h before anticipated discharge
### Cardiorenal AKI during diuresis
- Hold metformin during active AKI
- Reassess ACEi/ARB (e.g., lisinopril) decision during active diuresis
- Post-discharge BMP within 3-7 days when discharging on titrated diuretics with cardiorenal AKI
- Involve Nephrology if creatinine worsens or diuresis stalls
### Acute decompensated HF — workup of precipitant
- Always identify and document the precipitant ('why now'): dietary indiscretion, missed doses, recent illness, ischemic symptoms, arrhythmia
- Serial troponins q3-6h x2 in any HF exacerbation with known CAD and borderline troponin to rule out type 1/2 MI as trigger
- Review telemetry/ECG rhythm to exclude new arrhythmia (e.g., AFib with RVR) as precipitant
### HFrEF - GDMT optimization
- For HFrEF patients, verify presence of quadruple therapy: beta-blocker, ACE/ARB/ARNI, MRA, and SGLT2i; flag gaps for optimization
- Consider CRT-D eligibility evaluation for patients with EF ≤35% and LBBB
- For recurrent CHF admissions despite reported compliance, reassess adherence, dietary sodium, ischemia, and arrhythmia as precipitants
- Consider escalation of outpatient diuretic dose if progressive volume gain noted across admissions (rising discharge weights)
### CAD with borderline troponin
- In patients with known CAD presenting with dyspnea and borderline troponin, trend troponin I at 3 and 6 hours to rule out ACS as precipitant
- Compare current ECG to prior tracings to confirm any bundle branch block is truly old before attributing symptoms to non-ischemic cause
### Type 2 diabetes - inpatient management
- Hold metformin on admission for acute illness, rising creatinine, or potential contrast exposure (lactic acidosis risk)
- Bridge with correctional sliding scale insulin; check fingersticks QID
- Resume metformin at discharge if Cr returns to baseline and no contraindication
### Goals of care - recurrent admissions
- For patients with recurrent decompensations and prior declined interventions (e.g., revascularization), reconfirm goals of care each admission
- Consider palliative care if recurrent admissions and shift in patient preferences regarding aggressive interventions
### Heart failure exacerbation - discharge planning
- Home health referral for CHF disease management: daily weights, symptom check, med reconciliation, BP/HR monitoring
- Escalation criteria for home monitoring: weight gain >2 lb/day or >5 lb/week
- DME at discharge: home scale is essential for daily weights (note: scales generally not covered by Medicare, often out-of-pocket)
- Outpatient cardiology follow-up within 7-14 days post-discharge
- PCP follow-up within 1 week for BMP (K+, Cr) given diuretic intensification, especially in CKD
- Consider heart failure clinic / CHF disease management enrollment if available
- For recurrent CHF admissions, consider palliative care referral and revisit goals of care
### New structural valve findings on inpatient TTE
- When inpatient TTE reveals new moderate-or-greater MR with leaflet prolapse, document as a new structural diagnosis on the discharge summary and arrange repeat TTE in 3 months once euvolemic
- New mild-or-greater pulmonary hypertension (RVSP ≥40 mmHg) identified on inpatient TTE warrants outpatient cardiology surveillance and repeat imaging in 3 months
- Structural valve findings discovered during ADHF admission should trigger formal cardiology consult (not 'aware') and explicit structural valve clinic referral
### Sepsis fluid resuscitation
- Limit initial fluid bolus to 20mL/kg in patients with concomitant HF or ESRD.
  - Rationale: Standard 30mL/kg bolus carries a high risk of volume overload in susceptible populations [WikiID: d41bc0].
  - Evidence: Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021 [WikiID: d41bc0]
### Heart failure exacerbation - anemia workup
- In patients with HFrEF and anemia (Hgb < 12 g/dL), routinely order iron studies (ferritin, TSAT) and TSH as part of the precipitant/comorbidity workup.
  - Rationale: Anemia and thyroid dysfunction may contribute to symptoms or exacerbate HFrEF; evaluating these factors aids in comprehensive management and identifying potential triggers for recurrent admissions.
  - Evidence: General clinical practice for recurrent HF decompensation [WikiID: b9f1b7, WikiID: 6bddc0].
### Heart failure exacerbation - target dry weight setting
- When dry-weight creep is observed across admissions, set the new target dry weight below the most recent discharge weight (e.g., 3-4 kg below) rather than anchoring to the last documented dry weight.
  - Rationale: Anchoring to a creeping dry weight perpetuates inadequate decongestion; using a lower target accounts for likely under-diuresis on prior admissions.
### Loop diuretic safety in sulfa allergy
- When a patient with documented sulfa allergy has already received furosemide in the ED without a cross-reactivity check, do not defer the safety check — verify with pharmacy/attending BEFORE the next scheduled dose, and switch to ethacrynic acid if true reaction history.
  - Rationale: Continuing furosemide pending later review exposes the patient to repeated allergen doses; cross-reactivity must be resolved pre-dose, not flagged for later.
### HFrEF - beta-blocker uptitration deferral
- When patient is admitted on submaximal carvedilol dose (e.g., 12.5 mg BID, below target 25 mg BID for HFrEF), explicitly defer up-titration to outpatient cardiology once euvolemic and stable rather than initiating inpatient titration during active diuresis.
  - Rationale: Inpatient beta-blocker uptitration during active decongestion risks low-output state and hemodynamic instability; outpatient titration with stable volume status is safer.
  - Added: 2026-06-02
### ASA + prophylactic heparin co-administration
- When continuing home aspirin alongside prophylactic SQ heparin for VTE prophylaxis, confirm no active bleeding before sign-off; the combination is acceptable at prophylactic heparin dosing but warrants explicit documented assessment.
  - Rationale: Combined antiplatelet + anticoagulant therapy modestly elevates bleeding risk; explicit pre-sign-off check prevents oversight in patients with CAD on chronic ASA.
  - Added: 2026-06-02
### Recurrent ADHF - dry weight creep trajectory
- For patients with ≥3 ADHF admissions in 12 months with documented dry-weight creep across admissions, formally enroll in CHF disease management/transitional care clinic AND revisit code status/goals of care during the current admission — do not defer both to outpatient.
  - Rationale: Accelerating trajectory with dry-weight creep signals worsening disease; combining disease management enrollment with goals-of-care revisit captures both the medical and prognostic dimensions before the next decompensation.
  - Added: 2026-06-02
### Anemia in HFrEF - timing of workup
- Initiate anemia workup (iron studies, TSH) within 4 weeks of discharge via PCP for HFrEF patients with Hgb 11-12 g/dL; if iron-deficient, refer to cardiology for IV iron infusion given mortality/hospitalization benefit.
  - Rationale: Outpatient workup within a defined 4-week window prevents the anemia issue from being lost to follow-up while avoiding inpatient workup during active diuresis when values may be confounded.
  - Added: 2026-06-02

## Labs/Blood Work
### Sodium abnormalities
- Hyponatremia workup: serum osm, urine osm, urine sodium first
- Correction rate: no faster than 6-8 mEq/L per 24h (risk of osmotic demyelination)
- Always flag for PCP follow-up at 1 week post-discharge
### Cardiac biomarkers and electrolytes in ADHF
- Trend serial troponins in ADHF admissions, particularly with known ischemic cardiomyopathy
- Monitor magnesium and phosphate alongside BMP during active diuresis
### Cardiac biomarkers
- Repeat BNP prior to discharge in HF admissions to confirm return toward dry-weight baseline
- Borderline troponin (e.g., 0.04) in patient with known CAD requires serial trending, not dismissal
### Cardiorenal syndrome
- BUN/Cr ratio >20 suggests prerenal/cardiorenal component layered on CKD
- If creatinine continues to rise during diuresis, reassess volume status before slowing diuresis — cardiorenal often improves with decongestion
- Verify baseline creatinine from prior records to guide diuresis tolerance threshold
### Hypokalemia during loop diuresis
- Initiate KCl supplementation when K+ drops to low-normal (≤3.5) during active loop diuresis, especially with concurrent uptitration
- Continue KCl at discharge when discharging on higher-dose loop diuretic than home regimen; pair with 1-week post-discharge BMP
- Defer MRA initiation while patient requires exogenous KCl supplementation — reassess once K+ stable off supplementation or per cardiology
### BMP monitoring post-GDMT
- Check BMP at 1 week post-initiation of ARNI or MRA in HFrEF patients.
  - Rationale: High risk of hyperkalemia and AKI in patients with baseline CKD [WikiID: 2f6961].
  - Evidence: 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Guideline [WikiID: 2f6961]

## Imaging
### Incidental imaging findings
- Any incidental finding requiring follow-up must appear in transitional issues
- Specify the follow-up modality, timeframe, and responsible provider
- Do not leave incidental findings unaddressed
### Pulmonary nodule surveillance (Fleischner)
- Solid pulmonary nodules 4-6 mm in patients >35 years with no high-risk features: CT chest without contrast in 3 months per Fleischner Society guidance
- Compare any newly identified nodule against the most recent prior chest imaging to establish whether truly new
- Assign explicit responsible provider (typically PCP) to order surveillance CT and document timeframe anchored to date of index imaging

## Medications
### Anticoagulation
- Always verify indication, duration, and current INR (if warfarin) before discharge
- Bridge therapy decisions: discuss with attending

### Sulfa Allergy
- Always cross-reference reported sulfa allergies with sulfonamide-containing drugs (e.g., Furosemide) before administration
- Verify cross-reactivity risk with pharmacy or attending in cases of documented severe sulfa allergy (hives/anaphylaxis) [WikiID: 8350f7]
- Before initiating sulfonamide-derivative medications (e.g., Furosemide), always perform a safety check for potential cross-reactivity with documented sulfa allergies (hives/anaphylaxis).
- If cross-reactivity is confirmed or suspected, evaluate for alternative agents (e.g., ethacrynic acid for loop diuretics).
### DVT prophylaxis
- Address VTE prophylaxis on every admission: order pharmacologic ppx or explicitly document contraindication
- INR <1.5 and platelets >50 generally not a contraindication
### Metformin in hospitalized patients
- Hold metformin during admission if Cr is at/above baseline with anticipated further bump, acute illness, or planned contrast exposure (lactic acidosis risk)
### VTE prophylaxis
- Prefer unfractionated heparin (5000 units SQ q8h) over LMWH in patients with CKD3 or worse (Cr >1.5) due to LMWH accumulation
- All hospitalized HF patients with reduced ambulation require VTE prophylaxis unless contraindicated
### VTE prophylaxis in CKD
- Verify CrCl before dosing enoxaparin; if CrCl <30 mL/min, reduce to 30 mg SQ daily
- Consider unfractionated heparin 5000 units SQ q8h as renal-safe alternative when CrCl is borderline
### ACE inhibitors during diuresis
- Hold parameters for ACE inhibitor: Cr rise >30% above baseline or SBP <100
- Monitor potassium closely when ACE inhibitor combined with loop diuretic or MRA
### Metformin in acute kidney injury / CKD
- Hold metformin when creatinine is elevated (e.g., Cr 1.8) due to lactic acidosis risk
### Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) for HFrEF
- Optimize GDMT during admission: ARNI, MRA, SGLT2i
- ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) often requires prior authorization under Medicare Part D — verify formulary early to avoid discharge delay
- SGLT2i coverage variable under Part D — may require PA; verify before discharge
### Sulfa allergy - diuretic transition
- For patients with a documented severe sulfa allergy (hives/anaphylaxis), perform a documented safety check for potential cross-reactivity with sulfonamide-derivative diuretics (e.g., Furosemide) before administration.
  - Rationale: Standardization of safety screening to prevent potential allergic reactions.
  - Interpretation: If cross-reactivity is suspected, evaluate for alternative non-sulfonamide loop diuretics like ethacrynic acid [WikiID: 410372].
### Medication adherence verification
- For recurrent admissions where patient reports strict adherence, verify true adherence with pill counts and pharmacy refill history rather than relying on self-report alone.
  - Rationale: Self-reported adherence is unreliable in recurrent decompensations; objective verification is needed before attributing precipitant to non-adherence vs. other causes.
### Beta-blockers in diabetics
- When carvedilol or other beta-blockers are continued in T2DM patients on sliding-scale insulin, instruct nursing to monitor glucose objectively (fingersticks QID) rather than relying on adrenergic hypoglycemia symptoms.
  - Rationale: Beta-blockers mask adrenergic symptoms of hypoglycemia, increasing risk of unrecognized hypoglycemic events.
### ARNI transition from ACEi
- If switching from ACEi to ARNI per cardiology recommendation, require a 36-hour washout period and initiate Part D prior authorization workflow at least 48h before anticipated discharge.
  - Rationale: Concomitant ACEi/ARNI use causes angioedema risk; ARNI also has high PA likelihood under Medicare Part D and is a common cause of discharge delay.
### Inpatient PA flag interpretation
- Treat prior authorization flags on inpatient-only medications (e.g., SQ heparin, insulin lispro) as formulary-database artifacts rather than true coverage barriers, since inpatient meds are bundled under Part A DRG payment; document this explicitly in prescription notes.
  - Rationale: PA flags from formulary tools generate false positives for inpatient meds covered under bundled Part A payment, creating workflow noise; outpatient Part D PA only applies if medication is continued at discharge.
  - Added: 2026-06-02
- If discharge on insulin is anticipated, preemptively switch to regular insulin sliding scale (Tier 1 formulary) rather than lispro to avoid Part D PA delay at discharge.
  - Rationale: Lispro typically requires Part D PA or step therapy outpatient; early formulary substitution prevents discharge delay.
  - Evidence: [WikiID: 435009]
  - Added: 2026-06-02

## Cardiology

### Troponin elevation with LBBB or known CAD
- Order serial troponin q3-6h x2-3 to trend any mildly elevated troponin
- In patients with LBBB or known multivessel CAD, do not exclude type 2 MI/ACS based on a single troponin value
- Compare current ECG to prior ECG for any new changes despite LBBB
### ECG interpretation with LBBB
- When LBBB is present, characterize the underlying rhythm (sinus vs AFib vs paced) and compare to prior ECG — occult AFib changes anticoagulation strategy
- LBBB masks ischemic ECG changes — do not rely on ECG alone to exclude ACS
### Atrial Fibrillation
- For symptomatic AFib in patients >80 years old, defer rhythm control preference to cardiology or accept rate control as an alternative.
  - Rationale: Early rhythm control is preferred generally, but clinical nuance is required for the elderly to balance symptom control vs. procedure risk [WikiID: c94385].
  - Evidence: 2023 ACC/AHA AFib Guideline [WikiID: c94385]

## Documentation

### Code status / goals of care
- Document code status and goals-of-care on admission for any patient with accelerating disease trajectory (e.g., recurrent admissions for same condition) or previously declined major interventions

## Prophylaxis

### Inpatient prophylaxis checks
- Confirm DVT prophylaxis is addressed on every inpatient admission

## Discharge Planning

### Case management for CHF discharge
- Home health referral should include RN for CHF teaching (daily weights, 2g Na/2L fluid restriction), medication reconciliation, and post-discharge BMP draw within 3-7 days
- Recommend home scale as DME for all CHF discharges (note: Medicare typically does not cover — patient out-of-pocket)
- Verify inpatient vs observation status early when SNF disposition is possible (3-midnight rule)
- PT evaluation inpatient for patients with recurrent admissions or functional decline trajectory, especially if living alone
- Verify transportation to follow-up appointments before discharge
### Insurance and DME considerations
- Verify Medicare homebound status documentation before initiating home health
- Home PT covered under home health benefit if part of plan of care
- Scales typically not covered by Medicare — confirm patient can obtain one before discharge
- Substitute formulary alternatives early if PA risk identified (e.g., heparin SQ for enoxaparin, regular insulin for lispro)
### Follow-up appointment verification
- Cardiology and specialty follow-up appointments must be scheduled (booked) prior to discharge, not just referred
- Verify home health agency availability and start date prior to discharge
### Discharge medication dose verification
- Reconcile inpatient diuretic dosing (e.g., BID inpatient regimen) against discharge prescription to avoid carry-over dosing errors; explicitly state the consolidated outpatient dose and rationale
- Flag any discrepancy between Hospital Course narrative and Discharge Medications list as a critical safety issue requiring physician resolution before sign-off

## Transitional Issues

### Carry-forward issue tracking
- On admission, review prior transitional issues from previous discharges and verify whether they were completed (e.g., outstanding PCP labs, deferred specialist re-discussions)
- Flag unresolved prior transitional issues as active items in the current plan
